Testtesttest City_Nature on the Mur


Rebuilding processes of urban riverscapes and natural landscapes in Graz in history and present

In 2020, the riverscape in the city of Graz is being reconstructed into local recreation areas. Such an transformation of the river landscape in the city of Graz is an urban development process that can also be observed in other cities. What are the reasons for this trend in the urban development of reconstructing urban riverscapes, and building local recreation areas along rivers? The initial hypothesis for answering this question is the conception of the so-called River History, whereby the reasons go along with processes in the history of the urban industrial society, which had transformed natural rivers into Industrialized Rivers. Due to processes in the history of industrialization and urbanization, nature has been primarily considered as a utilizable resource for industry, and has been externalized to a place outside the city.
Today cities are increasingly not building against nature anymore, but with nature. I will show the contemporary process of urban development with nature by using the example of the new urban riverscape in Graz. My research will show: At the river Mur in Graz in 2020, there is an urban development process against and with nature at the same time taking place. This process can be seen as the construction of a second nature (Lefebvre) where nature is being integrated into the city as a design element of the urban space. Therefore the second nature in the city is both: a material nature, which plays an important role for the life in the city, but also a cultural artefact, which evokes nature.

Read the entire master's thesis in german language here: Stadt_Natur an der Mur: vergangene und gegenwärtige Herstellungsprozesse städtischer Fluss- und Naturlandschaften in Graz

Floating along the river Sava


Exploring the river Sava in Croatia by a-self made raft

The river Sava is 947 kilometres long and connects four countries, three capital cities and about 8 million people. Geographically, the River marks the norht beginning of the Balkans. But for the people who live along the Sava, the river is the heart of the Balkans. A unique natural environment and one of the last pieces of untouched nature in the middle of Europe. So we hit the (water) route to discover the European Amazonas with a self-made raft, made of wood and plastic barrels.
At the end of every summer, the water level of the Sava is at its lowest. The river flows calmly in towards the southeast and is easily navigable by rafts. But except for the low-water periods, the river Sava overflows its banks and turns into a torrential river. For this reason, the people have learned not to underestimate the water, but to appreciate nature. During our two-weeks trip, we also learned to appreciate the water of the river Sava. Her leisureliness, loneliness and peace stimulate us to discover nature and people.

Reconstructing Augarten


Insights behind the fences

The Augarten park in the city of Graz is a popular place, especially for the residents of the surrounding neighborhoods. The park is located in the center of Graz, next to the river Mur, which economically, socially and historically divides the city into two different parts.
When visiting the Augarten in February 2019, there were changes going on: At the northern end of the park, fences have been raised and the sounds of construction works can be heard. Today is the day when parts of the Augarten will be transformed into a new urban recreation area. A new river bay is to enhance the park and increase its popularity with a new riverside.
But first, the trees along the river have to be removed. It is noisy and loud work, because motor saws and power shovels are cutting and removing the trees brutaly. Meanwhile, the falling trees became a symbol of resentment in Graz. Because two years ago, a construction work began on a controversial new power station on the river Mur. And for this new power plant it was necessary to cut down trees and remove nature in the city in order to rebuild a new nature.

Abandoned Places


An unknown world is waiting just behind the door.

"First of all, there is this thrill: you are getting closer to the building, you are nervous, and you have no idea if there will be a security guard. You look for an entrance as you climb the wall or crawl under the fences. And when you find an entrance, you are literally crossing a threshold from our world into another, unknown world. It is a exiting feeling because you know that this one building felt out from time, and has some stories to tell about the past, the present and the future of the city: The past is the history of the building itselfs (...) The present you can find in the murals, the items and legacy from other urban explorers (...) And soon, in the future, this places will be demolished forever. Because the city is changing all the time to meet the current requirements of urban life. And so these places are also changing and will be vanished soon."

One Night on Train


Atmospheres between sunset and sunrise.

A night train is more than just a vehicle for transporting people from one place to another. Travelling with the night train also enables us to experience the night, the magical time between sunset and sunrise, in a unique way. On the train we are travel with strangers, it is a place where we can meet random people, share stories with them and unfould our thoughts. Or we can just listen to the sound of the wheels and fall asleep until we wake up in the morning in a different city or different country.

Berlin: Cuvrystraße


The Cuvry wasteland in Berlin. An informal settlement as a form of resistance.

Since the fall of the Berlin Wall, the city has changed, especially the district Kreuzberg. Not everyone in Kreuzberg agrees to these changes. Let's go for a walk in this disctrict!
"We are getting out of the underground station Schlesisches Tor. Here, in the streets of Kreuzberg, people are talking in different languages. We are heading on the Schlesische Straße, passing bars, clubs and restaraunts. In front of us, we can see two huge, about 15 meters large, murals on the walls. The are is enclosed by a wooden wall and bushes an the one side, and the river Spree on the another side. Highly attracted by these murals, we are passing a wooden gate. At the same time while we are stepping in, we are escaping from the noisy Schlesische Straße and find ourselves in another world: at the Cuvry wasteland. We can see some small houses and some tents (...) This area seems to be a living space, where people have built an informal village in the middle of the city. But also a place of resistance, fighting against gentrification in Kreuzberg. Let's hear the stories of these people (...)"

(c) R. Steinwender
